Risks of Death

  • All life involves risk
  • Degree of risk often subject to our own choices
  • Some risks identified only after years of exposure to hazardous condition
  • Individuals relate to risk-taking in different ways
  • Survivors may "blame the victim" as a way to cope with risky activities

According to Anthony Giddens "We live in a world where hazards created by ourselves are as, or more, threatening than those that come from outside."

 Domains of Risk

  1. Recreational and occupational activities (Karoshi – this refers to fatigue caused by long hours of work that exceed normal physiological limitations)
  2. Accidents and disasters
  3. Violence, homicide, terrorism (over 30% of arguments are over money, property, "romantic situation" etc)
  4. War and the nuclear threat
  5. AIDS and other emerging diseases

In some cases, we can exercise considerable choice about the nature and degree of risk to which we are exposed to e.g. drinking, smoking, driving etc. Risk can be minimized.

Risks: Recreational and Occupational Activities

  1. Activities in pursuit of the "good life"
  2. Thrill-seeking sometimes involves attempts to deny death or the fear of death
  3. Minimizing risk requires acknowledging threat and taking steps to compensate

 Accidents

  • Viewing accidents as chance events reduces the possibility of preventing their occurrence
  • Carelessness, ignorance, and lack of awareness often play a role in causing accidents
  • Unsafe environmental conditions contribute to accidents
  • Individuals and societies can minimize the risk of accidents

A wrongful death occurs when a person is killed due to the negligence or misconduct of another individual, company or entity. 

 Violence

  • Interpersonal violence is a public health problem
  • Nearly 15,000 murders occurred in the U.S. In 2/3 of the cases guns were used.
  • Young people are disproportionately represented among victims of violence
  • Children who witness violence are emotionally affected even when physically unharmed. They are usually the "silent victims"

 

The most threatening of violent acts are those that occur without apparent cause, with the victim selected seemingly at random

The use of violence to achieve personal or political goals is found worldwide

 War

Early European sociologists, such as Karl Marx, Max Weber and Emile Durkheim, were critically engaged with the effect of war on social development, and with the military's relationship with larger society. In fact, this, together with the growth of industrial capitalism, was central to their understanding of social change and modernity. Both Marx and Weber analysed the matter primarily in relation to the influence of the military and war on the modern capitalist state, albeit from different ontological perspectives. On the one hand, Marx's theory of social change inevitably implied an interest in the mechanics of collective violence by the state, which, according to him, was a mere extension of capitalist interest. On the other, there was the working class seeking to transform the social order through revolution, which inevitably implied confrontation with the state's military forces. As such, Marx saw capitalism as the root cause of war, and contingent upon the existence of class divisions. 

Of the three classical social theorists, Weber was most explicit in his explanations of the military as an instrument of power and force, emphasising the link between politics and violence, and the importance of the state's control over the monopoly of violence. He emphasised the centrality of the military to state power. For Weber, a state is only a state when it is able to hold the exclusive right to authorise the legitimate use of physical force within its territory (something that many African states lack). As such, he places the military at the core of his analysis of power, as the most coercive tool at the disposal of the state. Weber's conception of the monopoly of violence as a symbol of state strength is still relevant today, albeit challenged due to the power and influence of non-state actors, which have caused the state to lose control of the monopoly of violence. Nonetheless, Weber's measure of the legitimate control and management of violence to provide state security or protection to its citizens continues to be used as an indicator of state strength.

Durkheim was a pacifist who considered violence to be a largely irrational, anachronistic feature of life. With his emphasis on cohesion and the need to establish consensus in society, he appeared almost disinterested in the question of social conflict. This is not to say that he ignored the influence of the military and war on society, but this was a subtext in his work as stated by Mukherjee, "it was theorised by caveat, by silence and implied as present by its absence". Even though Durkheim's work never theorised violence, his understanding of the effect of anomie and social disintegration on society is of practical value, and enables us to discern the effect and consequences of war on society. Based on this, one can actually see how war actually creates large-scale anomie, destroys the social fabric of society, and can evoke states of normlessness.

Understanding how social capital is created is especially important within the context of war, for two reasons. While on the one hand, social capital is the substance that holds societies together and isessential to foster peace and development, on the other it can also generate conflict. Especially where bonds of trust are exploited along existing fault lines of race or ethnicity, it can fragment societiesand fuel wars on the basis of identity, as Kaldor points out. To understand the various dimensions of 'social capital', one is of course compelled to engage with the work of French sociologist Pierre Bourdieu,who refers to social capital as the invisible glue that enables societies to function. The concept, however, is best explained by Robert Putnam, who describes how different forms of social capital – bonding,bridging and linking capital – are created, and how necessary this is if societies are to build the required social and political cohesion for them to prosper. THE MILITARY, WAR AND SOCIETY: THE NEED FOR CRITICAL SOCIOLOGICAL ENGAGEMENT by Lindy Heinecken (2015)

 Terrorism

  • Involves use or threat of violence to create a mood of fear among both its direct victims and a wider audience
  • Aims to destroy the sense of security people normally feel in familiar places
  • Modern terrorism—A synthesis of war and theater

 

The Impact of Terrorism: Variables

  • Scope and extent of physical and emotional devastation
  • Realization that destruction and horror was intentional and directed not just at individuals but also at the government and culture they represent
  • Age of victims, especially when many are young adults or children
  • Defenselessness of the victims and suddenness of attack
  • Duration of the event, including rescue and recovery period
  • Difficulties and delays in identifying and returning remains of victims
  • Issues related to disposition of "common" or unassociated human tissue
  • Issues related to remains of terrorists mixing with remains of victims
  • Intrusiveness of media coverage, especially repetitive broadcast of disturbing visual images
  • Speculation about perpetrators and their motivations
  • Speculations about whether the true architects of the terrorism can be apprehended and brought to justice
  • Speculation about the ability of official agencies to have prevented the attack and resulting loss of faith in government to protect its citizens
  • Large-scale memorials and anniversary commemorations

 AIDS and Other Emerging Diseases

First cases of AIDS reported in 1981

Main epicenters have been U.S. and sub-Saharan Africa

The political and social response to AIDS has been mixed

 Despite therapeutic advances, the AIDS epidemic is not over

Emerging and re-emerging diseases remain threatening (e.g., Ebola, TB)

 

 Coping with Risks

Risks of various kinds confront us in daily life

"Horrendous death" typically has its origins in human activity

Reducing horrendous deaths requires confronting the desire to deny their reality

Survival of societies as well as individual requires finding adequate means of coping with risks
